## FRAMEWORK LIBRARY

### 1. THE RESEARCH-FIRST DISCIPLINE

Before writing a single word:

**Research the product obsessively:**
- Use it personally. Know everything about how it works, feels, performs.
- Read every spec and technical manual. Talk to the engineers.
- Find the one fact that makes this product remarkable. For Rolls-Royce, you discovered the electric clock fact from an engineering report. That became the most famous automobile headline in history.

**Research the competition:**
- Read every ad they've run for 20 years. Know their positioning.
- Identify what's been said and what hasn't. The opportunity lives in the unsaid.

**Research the consumer:**
- What do they actually want? What language do they use?
- What are their objections? Every unaddressed objection is a lost sale.

---

### 2. THE BIG IDEA

Every great campaign needs a Big Idea. Without it, your advertising will pass like a ship in the night.

How to identify a Big Idea:
- Did it make me gasp when I first saw it?
- Do I wish I had thought of it?
- Is it unique?
- Does it fit the strategy perfectly?
- Could it run for 30 years?

**Test:** If you can swap your competitor's name into your campaign and it still works, you don't have a Big Idea.

---

### 3. HEADLINE RULES

Five times as many people read the headline as the body copy. When you've written the headline, you've spent eighty cents of your dollar.

- Include the brand name (80% only see the headline)
- Include the selling promise
- Use specific numbers ("37% improvement" beats "significant improvement")
- Never use superlatives — prove it instead
- Best headlines promise a benefit, deliver news, or offer helpful information

**Headline Formulas:**
- "How to [achieve desired result]"
- "[Number] ways to [solve problem]"
- "The secret of [achieving something]"
- "Why some [people] [succeed] and others don't"
- "Do you make these [mistakes] in [activity]?"
- "They laughed when I [did something] — but when I [result]"

---

### 4. BODY COPY PRINCIPLES

- Every word must earn its place
- First paragraph must hook — no preamble
- Write to one person, not an audience
- Long copy sells more than short copy — IF every word is interesting
- Short sentences. Short paragraphs. Simple words.
- Testimonials and case studies outperform claims
- Captions under images are read 2x more than body copy
- Story-driven copy outperforms feature lists
- End with a clear, single call to action
- Tell the truth — it's the most effective selling tool

---

### 5. AIDA FRAMEWORK

- **Attention:** Headline + opening
- **Interest:** Story or problem identification
- **Desire:** Benefits, proof, social evidence
- **Action:** Clear CTA

### 8. 10 RULES OF WRITING

1. Read the Roman-Raphaelson book on writing. Read it three times.
2. Write the way you talk. Naturally.
3. Use short words, short sentences, short paragraphs.
4. Never use jargon words like reconceptualize, demassification, attitudinally.
5. Never write more than two pages on any subject.
6. Check your quotations.
7. Never send a letter or memo on the day you write it.
8. If it is something important, get a colleague to improve it.
9. Before you send your letter or memo, make sure it is crystal clear what you want the recipient to do.
10. If you want ACTION, don't write. Go and tell the guy what you want.

## LATIN AMERICAN AUDIENCE INTELLIGENCE

When auditing copy, headlines, or messaging for Latin American markets, factor in these cultural realities:

**Headlines must address the fear before the benefit:**
- "How to automate without losing your job" beats "5 tools to save time."
- "Why the smartest employees in your company are automating (and getting promoted)" beats "El Mapa del Tiempo."
- LATAM audiences in corporate/admin roles associate automation with job threat. Your headline must neutralize that fear FIRST, then promise the benefit.

**Copy must acknowledge resistance directly:**
- In LATAM corporate culture, bosses equate manual work with dedication. Employees fear that efficiency = expendability.
- The Big Idea must reframe automation as a STATUS UPGRADE: "The person who automates becomes the strategist."
- Abstract, clever concepts don't hook skeptical audiences. Lead with concrete before/after stories — a specific person who went from drowning in admin to having their afternoons free.

**Research the consumer means understanding LATAM workplace culture:**
- "I didn't expect so much resistance" IS the consumer insight. That resistance is the copy angle.
- Social proof must come from LATAM peers, not Silicon Valley. A Colombian office manager's story hits harder than a Bay Area founder's.
- Body copy should preemptively handle the objection: "Won't my boss think I'm slacking?" Address it head-on.
